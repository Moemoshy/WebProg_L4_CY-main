Plot Key Information Cast Creative Team Characters Reviews

Plot

The Truman Show is a show about a man named Truman who was born and sold to be a star of a show named "The Truman Show" where he is unaware of. He lives his life as normally as everyone around him make it to. His life is broadcasted to billions of people around the world 24/7, but one day, Truman notices something off about his day.

Creative Team

Director:

→Peter Weir – Acclaimed Australian filmmaker known for Dead Poets Society (1989) and Witness (1985). His vision brought a mix of drama, satire, and psychological depth to the film.

Writer:

→Andrew Niccol – Screenwriter and producer who originally envisioned The Truman Show as a dark sci-fi thriller set in New York. He later refined it into a more grounded satire with Peter Weir’s guidance. Niccol is also known for writing Gattaca (1997).

Producers:

→Scott Rudin – One of Hollywood’s most respected producers, known for films like No Country for Old Men (2007) and The Social Network (2010).

→Andrew Niccol – Also co-produced the film, helping shape its unique concept.

→Edward S. Feldman – Veteran producer involved in various successful projects.

Executive Producers:

→Lynn Pleshette – Helped with development and securing studio interest.

→Adam Schroeder – Assisted in production and studio coordination.

Cinematographer (Director of Photography):

→Peter Biziou – Award-winning cinematographer who used a mix of surveillance-style shots and traditional cinematography to enhance the film’s reality TV aesthetic.

Editor:

→William M. Anderson – His editing helped maintain the illusion of a live broadcast while balancing emotional moments.

Music Composer:

→Burkhard Dallwitz – Provided the film’s haunting and emotional score.

→Philip Glass – Contributed additional music, including pieces from his existing compositions, adding to the film’s surreal and dramatic feel.

Production Design:

→Dennis Gassner – Designed the picture-perfect world of Seahaven, inspired by master-planned communities like Seaside, Florida.

Costume Design:

→Marilyn Matthews – Created a wardrobe reflecting a 1950s-inspired, idealized American suburban aesthetic, reinforcing the artificial nature of Truman’s world.

Reviews

(Kendrick, 2023)

The Truman Show’s new 4K/Dolby Vision presentation offers a solid improvement over Paramount’s 2008 Blu-ray, which offered a decent transfer that was marred by too much DNR. The new 4K disc eliminates that problem, offering a presentation that is much more nuanced and film-like, with fine detail that accentuates the smooth textures of Seahaven’s constructed surfaces (as well as the ostentatious tweed suits that Truman wears) despite the film’s unnaturally bright lighting. The improved detail does work to the film’ detriment in its one major special effects shot, however, which reveals the Seahaven studio as an all-too-obvious matte painting. The color also appears to be more intense, with an emphasis on the deeply saturated primary colors that are so crucial to the film’s slightly unreal quality. Dark scene also fare very well, with strong blacks and good shadow detail. (A quick note on the aspect ratio: The transfer is matted at 1.85:1, the aspect ratio used on the 2005 Special Collector’s Edition DVD. The 2008 Blu-ray was matted at 1.78:1, while the original 1998 DVD transfer was matted at 1.66:1. I believe that Peter Weir intended it to be projected at 1.66:1 to more closely mimic a cathode ray television screen’s dimensions, but most—if not all—U.S. theaters projected it at 1.85:1, as is their custom for films shot flat.) The 4K disc also upgrades the soundtrack from Dolby Digital TrueHD 5.1 to Dolby Atmos, which has great depth and surround activity and also really shows off Burkhard Dallwitz’s musical score. The storm-at-sea sequence near the end of the film is particularly enveloping, with a good use of the low end and surrounds. Unfortunately, despite being billed as a “25th Anniversary Edition,” there are no new supplements on the 4K disc. Instead, all the extras are the same as were found on the 2008 Blu-Ray disc and the 2005 Special Collector’s Edition DVD. The two-part making-of documentary How’s It Going to End? The Making of The Truman Show covers all the major aspects of the film’s production, from the lengthy screenwriting period in which director Peter Weir had writer Andrew Niccol change quite a few things, to scouting locations, to the actual shooting. Put together, the two parts of the doc run roughly 40 minutes, although it’s not entirely clear why it was split up (to some extent, the first half tends to deal more with the ideas behind the film, whereas the second part deals more with the nuts and bolts of production). Interviews include Weir, producer Edward S. Feldman, and stars Laura Linney, Ed Harris, and Noah \ Emmerich. Carrey only appears in circa-1998 interviews, but Niccol is completely MIA. The film’s effectively subtle visual effects are covered in the 13-minute featurette “Faux Finishing: The Visual Effects of The Truman Show,” which includes interviews with several members of the visual effects crew. The disc is rounded out with four deleted scenes, a thorough photo gallery of production pics, a pair of trailers, and a pair of TV spots.
